Flight Delays Analysis Project

Analysis of the 2004-2005 ASA Flight Dataset

Introduction

This analysis explores patterns in flight delays using the extensive ASA Statistical Computing Data Expo dataset from Kaggle. The dataset contains information about flight arrivals and departures in the United States from 2004 to 2005, covering over 20 million commercial flights.

The key questions addressed in this analysis include:

  • What is the best time of day, day of the week, and time of year to fly to minimize delays?
  • Do older planes suffer more delays?
  • How have flight patterns between different locations changed over time?
  • Can we detect cascading failures as delays in one airport create delays in others?
  • Is it possible to build predictive models for flight delays?

Methodology

This study combined R and Python analytical approaches. R was used for initial data preparation and statistical modeling, while Python facilitated machine learning applications and complex visualizations. The analysis followed these steps:

  1. Data cleaning and preprocessing to handle missing values and outliers
  2. Exploratory data analysis to identify patterns in flight delays
  3. Statistical testing to validate findings
  4. Predictive modeling using machine learning techniques
  5. Visualization of results and key insights

Key Findings

Optimal Flight Times

Analysis reveals that morning flights (6AM-10AM) have consistently fewer delays than evening flights. This pattern holds true regardless of the day of the week or season.

Figure 1: Average delay times by hour of departure

Seasonal Patterns

Winter months (December-February) show significantly higher delay rates due to weather conditions, while September offers the most reliable flight times.

Figure 2: Monthly variation in flight delays

Aircraft Age Impact

Contrary to popular belief, our analysis found no statistically significant relationship between aircraft age and delay frequency. Modern maintenance practices appear to effectively neutralize age-related reliability issues.

Figure 3: Relationship between aircraft age and delay frequency

Airport Cascade Effects

We identified clear patterns of cascading delays, particularly among hub airports. A major delay at airports like ORD (Chicago) or ATL (Atlanta) typically results in a ripple effect across the network within 2-3 hours.

Figure 4: Visualization of delay cascades across the airport network

Predictive Model

Our machine learning model achieved 78% accuracy in predicting whether a flight would be delayed by more than 15 minutes. The most predictive features were:

  • Time of day
  • Day of week
  • Origin airport's average delay rate
  • Carrier's historical performance
  • Current weather conditions

Figure 5: ROC curve showing model performance

Conclusion

This analysis provides practical insights for travelers and airlines alike. By understanding the patterns in flight delays, passengers can make more informed decisions when booking flights, while airlines can optimize their scheduling and operations to minimize disruptions.

Future research could explore how recent changes in the airline industry and technological advancements have affected these patterns.
